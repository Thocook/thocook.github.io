## Key Courses

### ECON 421 — Econometrics I

**Portfolio relevance:** Core data science and forecasting foundation.

This course built on the classical linear regression model and introduced time series models involving stationary and non-stationary variables. Topics included random regressors, method of moments estimation, simultaneous equation models, VEC, VAR, ARCH, panel data, qualitative dependent variables, and limited dependent variables. It also continued the use of data management and statistical software.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Built a foundation in regression, time series, panel data, and applied statistical modelling.
- Developed the econometric reasoning used in forecasting, pricing analysis, and performance measurement.
- Learned to evaluate relationships in messy real-world data rather than relying only on descriptive reporting.

---

### ECON 324 — Economic Data Analysis

**Portfolio relevance:** Data analytics, business intelligence, and database work.

This course introduced techniques of data retrieval, manipulation, and analysis, with emphasis on national and international databases, database management, spreadsheets, and statistical software packages.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Developed practical skills in data collection, cleaning, database management, and statistical analysis.
- Built the foundation for later work in Power BI, Excel, Python, reporting pipelines, and analytics workflows.
- Learned to convert raw data sources into usable evidence for business decisions.

---

### ECON 325 — Mathematics for Economists I

**Portfolio relevance:** Mathematical foundation for modelling and optimization.

This course covered mathematical tools for economic studies, including linear models, basic matrix algebra, univariate calculus, exponential and logarithmic functions, comparative static analysis, and constrained and unconstrained optimization.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Built the mathematical base for economic modelling, optimization, and analytical problem-solving.
- Developed comfort with matrix algebra, calculus, and optimization concepts used in data science and pricing models.
- Strengthened the ability to reason formally about trade-offs, constraints, and model behaviour.

---

### ECON 326 — Mathematics for Economists II

**Portfolio relevance:** Advanced optimization and analytical modelling.

This course covered mathematical tools for advanced economic studies, including the implicit function theorem, multivariate calculus, constrained optimization with inequality constraints, the envelope theorem, and dynamic analysis.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Extended my quantitative toolkit into multivariate calculus, dynamic analysis, and constrained optimization.
- Built intuition for pricing, allocation, and decision problems where businesses face real constraints.
- Strengthened my ability to understand the mechanics behind models rather than treating them as black boxes.

---

### ECON 433 — Financial Economics

**Portfolio relevance:** Finance, risk, portfolio thinking, and asset pricing.

This course introduced the theory and practice of finance from an economist's point of view. Topics included decision-making under uncertainty, portfolio models such as CAPM and APT, equilibrium in financial markets, arbitrage, financial asset pricing, and derivative securities such as options.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Built a foundation in portfolio theory, risk, asset pricing, arbitrage, and decision-making under uncertainty.
- Informs my ability to frame business portfolios, pricing decisions, and performance trade-offs using risk-adjusted thinking.
- Connects naturally to pricing, revenue management, investment analysis, and marketplace strategy.

---

### ECON 461 — Industrial Organization

**Portfolio relevance:** Pricing, competition, market structure, and strategy.

This course examined departures from perfect competition and analyzed economic behaviour in industrial settings. Topics included market structure, theories of oligopoly, entry, product differentiation, advertising, technological change, vertical integration, monopoly, and merger issues.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Built a strong foundation in competitive strategy, market structure, pricing power, product differentiation, and advertising economics.
- Highly relevant to my work in Amazon pricing, advertising, e-commerce competition, and marketplace analytics.
- Helps explain how firms behave strategically when markets are imperfect and competitors react.

---

### ECON 464 — Game Theory, Information, and Economic Modelling

**Portfolio relevance:** Strategy, incentives, pricing decisions, and competitive behaviour.

This course introduced mathematical tools to model and analyze strategic interactions across economic, social, political, and international settings. Topics included non-cooperative and cooperative game theory, strategic and extensive form games, Bayesian games, coalition games, dominant strategies, Nash equilibrium, subgame perfection, Bayesian equilibrium, repeated games, auctions, bargaining, oligopoly games, entry deterrence, pricing strategies, and collusion.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Developed a formal toolkit for analyzing strategic behaviour, incentives, competition, and information gaps.
- Relevant to pricing strategy, marketplace dynamics, auctions, advertising, and product decisions.
- Supports my ability to think through second-order effects: how competitors, customers, and systems respond to a decision.

---

### ECON 465 — The Economics of Professional Sport

**Portfolio relevance:** Applied microeconomics, empirical analysis, and business of sports.

This course applied microeconomic theory to professional sports. Topics included earnings of professional sports stars, free agency and competitive balance, the value of professional sports teams to cities, and whether public subsidies for franchises or arenas are justified. It also investigated statistical evidence behind these explanations.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Applied economic theory and statistical reasoning to real-world sports markets.
- Explored labour markets, incentives, competitive balance, public finance, and valuation.
- Useful as a bridge between economics, analytics, business strategy, and sports/entertainment markets.

---

### ECON 492 — Advanced Urban Economics

**Portfolio relevance:** Spatial economics, cities, location choice, and market failures.

This course examined geographic aspects of economies through microeconomic theory. Topics included why, how, and where cities are created and organized, market failure at the city level, location choice, suburbanization, New Economic Geography, city-size distribution, geographic mobility, spatial sorting, and quality-of-life indices.

**How to describe this in my portfolio:**

- Developed an understanding of spatial economics, city formation, location choice, and urban market failures.
- Relevant to real estate analytics, geographic segmentation, local market strategy, and location-based decision-making.
- Connects economic theory with practical issues around cities, mobility, quality of life, and development.
