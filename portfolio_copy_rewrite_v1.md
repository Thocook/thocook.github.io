# Forecast-Adaptive Pricing

- Short title: Pricing
- Company: Trellis
- Year: 2025
- Dates: May 2025 – Present
- Role: Pricing Product Owner work across algorithm logic, product requirements, client adoption, and performance analysis.
- Themes: Algorithms & Decision Logic
- Tags: Pricing, Forecasting, WMAPE, Amazon Marketplace, Product Ownership

## Visual
- Nodes: WMAPE, SKU, PRICE
- Label: Forecast signals, SKU performance, and price movement connected into one measurable pricing loop.

## Tool / Concept Groups
- Signals: Forecast, Sales velocity, Inventory, Price bounds
- Measurement: WMAPE, Margin impact, ROI, Sales-to-plan
- Product Work: Requirements, Decision rules, Explainability, Client analysis
- Domain: Amazon marketplace, SKU catalogs, Pricing strategy

## Tabs

### Overview
**Headline:** Pricing that keeps sales closer to plan.

Amazon catalogs move fast. Forecasts, inventory, margin, and demand do not always move together. I helped build pricing logic that used forecast deviation, SKU performance, and pricing rules to make daily price changes more targeted, explainable, and measurable.

**Metrics:**
- 18x — estimated ROI on discount efficiency
- 4.5% — margin improvement / less discount required
- 12% — WMAPE improvement, from roughly 48% to 36%

**Bullets:**
- Treated pricing as a control layer for demand, not just a lever for revenue.
- Connected algorithm behaviour to business outcomes like WMAPE, margin, sales-to-plan, and discount efficiency.
- Supported client-facing analysis for a $400M+ Amazon operator managing 18,000+ SKUs.

### Logic
**Headline:** The real problem was not price. It was predictability.

At catalog scale, manual pricing reacts too late. Some products sell too quickly and create stockout risk. Others lag and tie up inventory. The pricing engine helped identify when to raise, lower, or hold price based on how each SKU was tracking against plan.

**Bullets:**
- Overperforming SKUs could support price increases when demand stayed strong.
- Underperforming SKUs needed selective demand recovery, not blanket discounting.
- On-plan SKUs needed stability, not unnecessary noise.
- WMAPE mattered because the business cared about weighted forecast adherence, not just top-line sales.

### Architecture
**Headline:** Forecast → signals → pricing → measurement.

The system connected forecast expectations, recent demand, inventory position, price bounds, and performance measurement into a controlled pricing loop. The goal was not to make mysterious model decisions. The goal was to make pricing logic explainable enough for operators and executives to trust.

**Bullets:**
- Forecast signal: expected sales rate and target sell-through plan.
- Demand signal: sales velocity, conversion behaviour, and deviation from plan.
- Pricing signal: current price, recent price changes, price bounds, and discount constraints.
- Measurement layer: WMAPE, margin impact, ROI, sales-to-plan, and discount efficiency.

### Next Steps
**Headline:** The next layer is smarter adaptation.

The engine could become stronger with richer elasticity estimates, competitor context, advertising interaction, seasonality, and controlled experimentation.

**Bullets:**
- Segment elasticity by category, price band, brand strength, and competitive position.
- Separate price effects from advertising, promotion calendars, and traffic shifts.
- Add explanation cards that show why each SKU moved up, moved down, or held.
- Compare algorithmic interventions against manual pricing workflows through controlled tests.

- Short title: RevOps
- Company: Trellis
- Year: 2025
- Dates: 2025
- Role: Marketing science and revenue operations work connecting website analytics, paid media, forms, and pipeline context.
- Themes: Growth & Revenue Systems

## Visual
- Nodes: GA4, LOOK, CRM
- Label: Website behaviour, landing-page analysis, and HubSpot pipeline context joined into one funnel view.

- Analytics: GA4, Looker, Landing pages, Source / medium
- CRM: HubSpot forms, HubSpot contacts, Leads, Deals
- Channels: Google Ads, Bing Ads, Amazon DSP, LinkedIn Ads
- Workflow: Form capture, Pipeline context, Outbound follow-up

## Tabs

### Overview
**Headline:** A clearer path from source to opportunity.

Traffic is useful, but pipeline is better. This project connected GA4, Looker, paid channels, landing pages, and HubSpot so marketing and outbound teams could better understand which sources were creating real sales opportunities.

**Metrics:**
- GA4 — source, session, campaign, and landing-page behaviour
- HubSpot — forms, contacts, leads, deals, and pipeline context
- 4+ — paid channels: Google Ads, Bing Ads, Amazon DSP, LinkedIn Ads

**Bullets:**
- Moved reporting beyond traffic volume toward lead creation, pipeline context, and outbound follow-up.
- Used Looker mainly for GA4 and landing-page analysis while HubSpot served as the CRM operating layer.
- Compared paid sources using website behaviour and HubSpot conversion context, not only ad-platform metrics.

### Logic
**Headline:** The best question was not “who clicked?” It was “what happened next?”

Marketing activity lived across GA4, paid platforms, landing pages, HubSpot forms, and HubSpot pipelines. Each tool showed part of the story. The work was about connecting those parts into a funnel that sales and marketing could actually discuss together.

**Bullets:**
- Website sessions did not automatically reveal lead quality or pipeline value.
- Landing-page performance needed to connect to form submissions and sales interest.
- HubSpot forms captured demand, but needed stronger source and campaign context.
- Outbound follow-up improved when reps understood which page, form, or campaign created the lead.

### Architecture
**Headline:** Source → session → landing page → form → lead → pipeline.

GA4 captured website behaviour, Looker supported analysis, HubSpot forms captured conversion activity, and HubSpot CRM connected that activity to leads, deals, and outbound workflows.

**Bullets:**
- GA4 captured source / medium, campaign, session, engagement, and landing-page behaviour.
- Looker surfaced GA4 and landing-page performance for analysis.
- HubSpot forms created or updated contacts and lead records.
- HubSpot deal pipelines supported outbound prioritization, follow-up, and opportunity tracking.

### Next Steps
**Headline:** Stronger attribution would make the funnel sharper.

The next layer would improve source matching, lead scoring, stage conversion reporting, and practical handoff rules between marketing and outbound sales.

**Bullets:**
- Improve campaign-to-contact attribution across paid sources and HubSpot records.
- Score leads using source, form, landing page, company attributes, and engagement behaviour.
- Build pipeline-stage conversion reporting by campaign and landing page.
- Create outbound prioritization views directly inside HubSpot.

# Trading R&D

- Short title: Trading R&D
- Company: Private Equity
- Year: 2024
- Dates: 2024
- Role: Quantitative finance research project using Pine Script, TradingView, technical signals, and backtest evaluation.
- Themes: Algorithms & Decision Logic
- Tags: Pine Script, TradingView, Backtesting, Sharpe, Sortino

## Visual
- Nodes: RSI, PINE, TEST
- Label: Technical signal logic translated into Pine Script rules and evaluated through TradingView backtesting.

## Tool / Concept Groups
- Platform: TradingView, Pine Script, Strategy tester
- Signals: Stochastic oscillator, RSI, 4-period SMA
- Risk Rules: 0.4% stop loss, 1.8% take profit, Layered exits
- Evaluation: Sharpe, Sortino, Trade count, Net profit

## Tabs

### Overview
**Headline:** Turning market intuition into testable rules.

This was a quantitative finance R&D project: a technical trading idea translated into Pine Script, tested in TradingView, and evaluated through backtest metrics with clear caveats around simulated performance.

**Metrics:**
- 32.2% — simulated net profit before commission and slippage
- 184 — backtested S&P 500 Futures trades
- 6.154 — reported Sortino ratio

**Bullets:**
- Designed long and short entry logic using stochastic oscillator zones, RSI behaviour, and short-term moving-average direction.
- Evaluated the strategy on S&P 500 Futures from 2023-06-01 to 2024-04-26 using a simulated $100K account.
- Presented results as research output, with commission and slippage excluded.

### Logic
**Headline:** The value was the research process, not the headline return.

The goal was to move beyond visual chart reading by turning a trading hypothesis into programmable logic. The point was not to claim a live trading system. The point was to build, test, measure, and understand the limits of the result.

**Bullets:**
- Long entries used upward momentum and oversold stochastic conditions.
- Short entries used downward momentum and overbought stochastic conditions.
- RSI rate of change and short-term moving-average direction acted as confirmation filters.
- Layered exit nets reduced dependence on one brittle reversal threshold.

### Architecture
**Headline:** Hypothesis → signal rules → Pine Script → backtest → review.

The strategy used stochastic D ranges, RSI behaviour, moving-average direction, and defined stop-loss / take-profit parameters to create rule-based long and short setups.

**Bullets:**
- Oversold stochastic D range: 3 to 24.
- Overbought stochastic D range: 78 to 84.
- Long exits used stochastic crosses under 94, 78, and 62.
- Evaluation used net profit, trade count, Sharpe, Sortino, average trade, and win/loss ratio.

### Next Steps
**Headline:** The next step is realism.

More serious testing would require transaction costs, out-of-sample periods, walk-forward validation, regime stress tests, and baseline comparisons.

**Bullets:**
- Add commission, slippage, and spread assumptions.
- Run walk-forward validation and out-of-sample tests.
- Stress test parameters across market regimes.
- Compare against simple baselines and risk-adjusted benchmarks.
